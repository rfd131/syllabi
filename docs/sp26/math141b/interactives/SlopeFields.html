Reading a slope field
A few habits make these plots much easier to interpret quickly.

What the ticks mean

Each short segment is drawn at a point (x, y) with slope f(x, y). If a solution passes through that point, its graph must be tangent to that segment. Reading a slope field is mostly a matter of letting your eye glide along the ticks, following the local slope.

Nullclines

The nullcline is the curve in the plane where f(x, y) = 0. Along it, ticks are horizontal — any solution crossing it has a momentary horizontal tangent. For autonomous equations, nullclines are horizontal lines.

Equilibria (autonomous)

When f depends only on y, horizontal lines y = y* with f(y*) = 0 are constant solutions. Stable equilibria attract nearby trajectories (f'(y*) < 0); unstable ones repel them (f'(y*) > 0). Look at the arrows immediately above and below each candidate line.

Matching a field to an ODE

Ask a few quick questions. Does the slope change along a horizontal slice (fixed y)? If not, the equation is autonomous. Are ticks symmetric under x → -x or y → -y? That constrains the form of f. Where do ticks become horizontal, vertical, or switch sign? Those are the nullclines — match them to the zero set of your candidate f.
