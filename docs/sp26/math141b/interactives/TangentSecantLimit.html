Tangent line as a limit of secants

Pick a function f, pick a base point x0, and watch the secant line through P = (x0, f(x0)) and Q = (x0+h, f(x0+h)) rotate toward the tangent as h → 0. The secant slope is the average rate of change of f over [x0, x0+h]; its limit — when it exists — is the instantaneous rate of change f′(x0).

Why this works
Four ideas the picture encodes.

Definition of the derivative

The instantaneous rate of change of f at x0 is

f ′(x0) = limh → 0 [f(x0+h) − f(x0)] / h.

Each value of h gives one secant slope; the derivative is what those slopes settle down to as h shrinks.

Secant slope = average rate of change

For a fixed h, the quotient [f(x0+h) − f(x0)] / h is exactly the average rate of change of f over the interval [x0, x0+h]: total change in output divided by total change in input.

So the limit sends average rates of change to an instantaneous one — the secant picture and the rate-of-change picture are the same picture.

One-sided limits must agree

A two-sided limit exists only if both one-sided limits exist and are equal. For the secant slope this means: the slopes of secants coming from the right (h > 0) and from the left (h < 0) must approach the same number.

Try the preset |x| at x0 = 0. The right-hand secants all have slope +1, the left-hand secants all have slope −1, and the two-sided limit does not exist — there is no single tangent line at the corner.

Geometry: rotation into tangency

As Q slides toward P along the curve, the line PQ pivots about P. If the curve is smooth at P, the pivoting line settles onto a single limiting line — the tangent.

At cusps, corners, or vertical tangents, the pivot does not settle onto one line, and the derivative fails to exist. The table and convergence plot catch this algebraically.
